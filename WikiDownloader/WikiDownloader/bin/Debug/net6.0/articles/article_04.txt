<p>The <b>Battle of Orewin Bridge</b> (also known as the <b>Battle of Irfon Bridge</b>) was fought between <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> (led by the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">Marcher Lords</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Welsh</a> armies on 11 December 1282 near <a href="/wiki/Builth_Wells" title="Builth Wells">Builth Wells</a> in mid-Wales. It was a decisive defeat for the Welsh because their leader, <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a> was killed, and this effectively ended the autonomy of Wales.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_of_the_War">Background of the War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Orewin_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background of the War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I" title="Conquest of Wales by Edward I">Conquest of Wales by Edward I</a></div>
<p>Llywelyn had already fought a war against <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a> in 1277. Edward had organised a large full-time army with which he overran all North Wales as far west as the <a href="/wiki/Conwy_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Conwy River">Conwy River</a>, and a fleet with which he captured <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>, depriving the Welsh of much of their grain. Llywelyn was forced to come to humiliating terms, and ceded large areas of Wales to England.
</p><p>Over the five years which followed, there was continued tension between Llywelyn and Edward over various lawsuits, and increasing unrest between the Welsh people and their English administrators in the newly transferred areas. The revolt was actually begun in 1282 by Llywelyn's brother <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Gruffydd" title="Dafydd ap Gruffydd">Dafydd</a>, who had sided with Edward five years earlier but now captured <a href="/wiki/Hawarden_Castle_(medieval)" title="Hawarden Castle (medieval)">Hawarden Castle</a> and slaughtered its garrison. This was followed by uprisings in many parts of Wales, and Llywelyn declared war on Edward on behalf of all Welsh.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign_up_to_December">Campaign up to December</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Orewin_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Campaign up to December">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edward repeated his tactics of the previous war, laboriously occupying North Wales as far as the Vale of Conwy and occupying Anglesey, while sending armies into southern and central Wales to overawe or subdue Llewelyn's allies and potential supporters. At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Llandeilo_Fawr" title="Battle of Llandeilo Fawr">Battle of Llandeilo Fawr</a> the English army attacking south Wales was ambushed and destroyed by the southern Welsh. On 6 November, Edward's lieutenant in Anglesey, <a href="/wiki/Luke_de_Tany" title="Luke de Tany">Luke de Tany</a>, launched a premature attack across a <a href="/wiki/Bridge_of_boats" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridge of boats">bridge of boats</a> which spanned the <a href="/wiki/Menai_Strait" title="Menai Strait">Menai Strait</a>. His force was ambushed, cut off and slaughtered at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moel-y-don" title="Battle of Moel-y-don">Battle of Moel-y-don</a>.
</p><p>These unexpected reverses undoubtedly set Edward back several months. He was forced to pause to raise fresh armies and supplies. Llywelyn took advantage of the respite by making an expedition into mid-Wales, to inspire and rally support. This was the territory nominally ruled by <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lord" title="Marcher Lord">Marcher</a> Lords, who enjoyed considerable independence from Edward. Some were wavering and prepared to support Llywelyn. However, three of them, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Lestrange" title="Roger Lestrange">Roger l'Estrange</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Giffard_(died_1299)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Giffard (died 1299)">John Giffard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Mortimer,_2nd_Baron_Mortimer" class="mw-redirect" title="Edmund Mortimer, 2nd Baron Mortimer">Edmund Mortimer</a> were staunch supporters of Edward, and were in the field in mid-Wales with an army consisting mainly of archers from Shropshire and some heavy cavalry, as well as troops donated to them by the Welsh lord <a href="/wiki/Gruffydd_ap_Gwenwynwyn" title="Gruffydd ap Gwenwynwyn">Gruffydd ap Gwenwynwyn</a>, who was an old enemy of Llywelyn. The English are thought to have had an army consisting of around 5000 infantrymen and 1300 heavy cavalry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_battle">The battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Orewin_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 11 December, Llywelyn's army occupied a hillside north of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Irfon_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Irfon River (page does not exist)">Irfon River</a> near the village of <a href="/wiki/Cilmeri" title="Cilmeri">Cilmeri</a>, placed to repel any attack from the south across Orewin Bridge. The army is thought to have consisted of a few thousand spearmen and javelinmen from North Wales, with some men-at-arms from Llywelyn's own <i>teulu</i> (household), and some local archers from <a href="/wiki/Brecon" title="Brecon">Brecon</a> (who had betrayed their former English allies and joined Llywelyn, having been disappointed in the English failure at the Battle of Llandeilo Fawr). Altogether, the army is thought to have added up to around 7000 infantry and 160 Cavalry (Llywelyn's Teulu). Llywelyn himself was not present, having gone to speak with local leaders (possibly at Builth Castle).
</p><p>A local inhabitant had told the Marchers about a ford across the Irfon two miles downstream, near its confluence with the <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">River Wye</a>, and they sent most of their archers across it to attack the Welsh in the flank. The Welsh army turned to face them, and the English mounted men-at-arms charged across the now undefended bridge.
</p><p>Meanwhile, the English archers shot into the Welsh spear <a href="/wiki/Sheltron" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheltron">schiltrons</a>, weakening and disorganising the troops. The English heavy cavalry then charged the rear of the army. The leaderless and demoralised Welsh were routed.
</p><p>As the Welsh army fled, Llywelyn returned in haste. On the outskirts of the fighting, he was attacked and cut down by an English man-at-arms named Stephen de Frankton, an English <a href="/w/index.php?title=Centenar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Centenar (page does not exist)">centenar</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere,_Shropshire" title="Ellesmere, Shropshire">Ellesmere, Shropshire</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Orewin_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Llywelyn's body was not recognised until the next day (he had probably gone in secret or incognito to his meeting, and would therefore not have worn any surcoat or other heraldic device). His head was cut off, and taken to London to be exhibited. He left only an infant daughter, <a href="/wiki/Gwenllian_of_Wales" title="Gwenllian of Wales">Gwenllian of Wales</a>. Leadership of the Welsh fell to Dafydd, who led a guerrilla resistance for some months but was soon betrayed, captured and executed as a traitor. Edward was able to formally end the existence of an organised resistance to English rule in the Welsh regions.
</p>
